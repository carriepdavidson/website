# Lead-Magnet Roadmap: carriepdavidson.com

*Compiled 2026-07-12. For Carrie Davidson, BSPH, BSN, RN. Eight future lead-magnet concepts, each specced for honest, modest promises and clean delivery. Voice canon applies: neuroscience precision, poetic reframe, no fear-based or diagnostic framing, no em-dashes. Coaching is not therapy; education is not medical care.*

## Guiding principles (read first)

- **Default is open access.** All 8 existing free resources (Trauma Tree Worksheet, Befriending Your Nervous System Workbook, ADHD Affirmation Cards, You're Not Broken ADHD ebook, Conscious Creation Workbook, Small Leavings Workbook, Small Leavings Journal, 3-minute quiz) are open downloads with no email wall. New magnets default to open too. Never use deceptive gating (no fake scarcity, no "enter email to unlock" on something implied to be free). If a resource invites an email, the value must be honestly framed as "get this plus the letter," and the download should still be reachable.
- **Truthful, modest promises only.** A reflection guide helps you notice a pattern. It does not cure trauma, regulate your nervous system for you, or replace care. Every magnet carries the standing scope disclaimer.
- **Platform reality (important).** The newsletter runs on Substack. **Substack has no automation or drip-sequence feature.** It sends broadcasts and welcome-email(s), not timed multi-day sequences triggered per subscriber. Any "5-day series" therefore cannot run natively on Substack. Options, flagged per concept below: (a) deliver the whole series as a single downloadable PDF/guide (works today, open access, zero automation); (b) move email automation to a tool that supports sequences (MailerLite, ConvertKit/Kit, Kajabi, which Carrie already owns) and run the drip there; (c) manual scheduled sends, which is labor-intensive and not recommended beyond a one-off.
- **Which future magnets should invite email, and why.** The existing 8 are all open, which is right for top-of-funnel trust with a trauma-sensitive audience. Recommend inviting (not requiring) email only where the format is inherently email-native or where ongoing delivery is the value: the 5-day series (email is the medium) and, optionally, the relationships workbook (higher-intent, deeper commitment). Keep the quick-reference and first-aid style guides fully open, because a person mid-trigger should never hit a form.

## 1. The Pattern Map

- **Intended audience:** therapy veterans who understand their patterns but cannot break them ("insight-rich and transformation-poor"); high-achieving women.
- **Core problem:** they see individual failures, not the single underlying loop.
- **Promise (truthful, modest):** a one-page map to see your recurring pattern as one whole system (trigger, state, behavior, cost), so it stops feeling like a hundred separate failures. It helps you notice; it does not fix.
- **Contents outline:** the four-part loop diagram; guided prompts for each part; one worked example; a gentle "what to do with what you noticed" close pointing to the Method (Witness step).
- **Topic cluster:** trauma-patterns
- **Best placement:** companion download on article F4 (What a Trauma Pattern Actually Is) and F17 (self-sabotage); Resources page library.
- **Follow-up pathway:** on download, invite to the Sunday Letter (Substack broadcast). No sequence needed.
- **Clinical review:** Yes, light. Verify the loop model language is descriptive, not diagnostic.
- **Access:** Open. (Sibling to the Trauma Tree Worksheet; keep it frictionless.)

## 2. Emotional Flashback First-Aid Guide

- **Intended audience:** people who experience sudden waves of old emotion with no clear trigger (CPTSD-adjacent).
- **Core problem:** an emotional flashback is frightening and they do not have plain, calm steps in the moment.
- **Promise (truthful, modest):** a short, calm reference of grounding steps to come back to the present. It is comfort and orientation, not treatment, and not a substitute for a therapist or crisis care.
- **Contents outline:** a two-line "what is happening to me" reassurance; 4 to 5 grounding practices in plain steps; a "this is not an emergency vs this is, here is who to call" box that surfaces the 988 / Crisis Text Line details; when to reach for professional support.
- **Topic cluster:** cptsd
- **Best placement:** companion on article F5 (Emotional Flashbacks); linked from the Resources library.
- **Follow-up pathway:** optional Sunday Letter invite after download. No sequence.
- **Clinical review:** Yes, required and thorough. Grounding steps and the crisis-vs-not guidance must be clinically checked; must not read as treatment or promise symptom control.
- **Access:** Open, and must stay open. A person mid-flashback must never meet an email form.

## 3. Why Calm Feels Unsafe (5-day email series)

- **Intended audience:** readers whose nervous system treats calm as a threat (directly extends published essay E9).
- **Core problem:** they are told to "just relax" and calm itself triggers alarm.
- **Promise (truthful, modest):** five short daily notes on why calm can feel unsafe and small ways to build tolerance for it. Understanding and gentle practice, not a cure.
- **Contents outline:** Day 1 why calm can feel like a warning; Day 2 the familiar-over-safe mechanism; Day 3 one small titration practice; Day 4 what regulation is and is not; Day 5 choosing the next small step (soft handoff to quiz/coaching).
- **Topic cluster:** nervous-system
- **Best placement:** email-invited signup on articles F3 and F16; Resources page.
- **Follow-up pathway (PLATFORM FLAG):** **Substack cannot run a 5-day triggered sequence.** Recommended: run this on **Kajabi (already owned) or MailerLite/Kit**, where per-subscriber drip automation exists; roll subscribers into the Sunday Letter afterward. Fallback if she wants it live this week with zero new tooling: ship the same five days as one open PDF "5-Day Guide" and skip automation. Do not attempt manual daily sends beyond a pilot.
- **Clinical review:** Yes. Verify the titration/tolerance framing; non-prescriptive.
- **Access:** Email-invited (email is the medium here, so this is honest, not gating). Offer the PDF fallback as an open alternative so no one is forced to subscribe.

## 4. Hypervigilance or Intuition? A Reflection Guide

- **Intended audience:** women who cannot tell a trauma-driven alarm from a true gut signal.
- **Core problem:** they either override real intuition or act on false alarms, and cannot tell which is which.
- **Promise (truthful, modest):** reflection prompts to help you notice the felt difference between a threat-scan and a genuine signal. A thinking tool, not a rule that tells you what is true.
- **Contents outline:** side-by-side "what hypervigilance tends to feel like vs what a settled signal tends to feel like"; 6 to 8 journal prompts; a "when in doubt, slow down" close; explicit note that only you (and, where relevant, a clinician) can interpret your own experience.
- **Topic cluster:** nervous-system
- **Best placement:** companion on F2 (Hypervigilance vs Anxiety); Resources library.
- **Follow-up pathway:** Sunday Letter invite on download. No sequence.
- **Clinical review:** Yes, light. Ensure it never tells the reader their perception is wrong or right; keep it reflective.
- **Access:** Open.

## 5. The Trauma Pattern Vocabulary Guide

- **Intended audience:** people newly finding words for experiences they could not name (over-explaining, fawning, freeze, emotional flashback, hypervigilance).
- **Core problem:** without shared language, the pattern stays invisible and self-blame fills the gap.
- **Promise (truthful, modest):** plain-language definitions of common trauma-pattern terms, so you can name what you notice. Vocabulary, not diagnosis.
- **Contents outline:** 10 to 15 short glossary entries, each: the term, a plain "what it can feel like," one non-clinical example, and a reframe line. Header note: naming a pattern is not diagnosing yourself.
- **Topic cluster:** trauma-patterns
- **Best placement:** evergreen reference linked from most foundational articles (F1, F5, F6, F7, F9); Resources library. Strong AEO asset (each entry is an extractable unit).
- **Follow-up pathway:** Sunday Letter invite. No sequence.
- **Clinical review:** Yes, required. Every definition source-verified; explicit non-diagnostic disclaimer given the glossary format invites self-labeling.
- **Access:** Open.

## 6. A Gentle Guide to Coming Back After a Trigger

- **Intended audience:** anyone in the hours after being triggered, flooded, or shut down.
- **Core problem:** the acute wave passes but they are left raw, ashamed, and unsure how to re-enter their day.
- **Promise (truthful, modest):** a short, kind guide to the after: re-orienting, self-compassion, and small re-entry steps. Companionship for the comedown, not treatment.
- **Contents outline:** "the wave has passed, here is the after"; 3 re-orienting practices; a self-compassion script that replaces the shame spiral; a "one small next thing" step; crisis resources box.
- **Topic cluster:** nervous-system
- **Best placement:** companion on F5 and F16; Resources library. Pairs with magnet #2 (first-aid = during; this = after).
- **Follow-up pathway:** Sunday Letter invite. No sequence.
- **Clinical review:** Yes, required. Practices and the shame-to-compassion framing clinically checked; non-prescriptive; surfaces crisis lines.
- **Access:** Open, and stay open (same reasoning as #2: acute-adjacent, no form).

## 7. The Creative Regulation Toolkit

- **Intended audience:** readers who have some regulation and want the "then what" (creativity as a regulation and meaning practice); ties to the Conscious Creation phase and essay E12.
- **Core problem:** they have learned to calm down but do not know how to build a life from the calmer place.
- **Promise (truthful, modest):** simple creative practices that support regulation and self-expression. A starting toolkit, not art therapy and not treatment.
- **Contents outline:** why creativity supports a settled state (one sourced paragraph); 5 to 7 low-barrier creative practices; a weekly rhythm suggestion; handoff to the Conscious Creation Workbook and the Rooted & Rising course.
- **Topic cluster:** creativity-healing
- **Best placement:** companion on R4 (Conscious Creation) and E12; Resources library.
- **Follow-up pathway:** Sunday Letter invite; warm cross-sell to Conscious Creation coaching/course.
- **Clinical review:** Yes, light. Distinguish clearly from clinical art therapy; verify the regulation-and-creativity claim with a source.
- **Access:** Open.

## 8. CPTSD and Relationships Reflection Workbook

- **Intended audience:** people whose patterns show up hardest in relationships (people-pleasing, over-explaining, attracting the familiar, the small leavings); ties to F7, F9, L5, and essay E5.
- **Core problem:** they repeat relational dynamics they can see but cannot change, and blame themselves.
- **Promise (truthful, modest):** guided reflection on how your patterns show up with others, and small ways to stay present instead of leaving yourself. Insight and gentle practice, not couples therapy or a fix.
- **Contents outline:** how survival patterns show up relationally (fawn, over-explain, familiar-over-safe); reflection prompts across sections; a "small leavings" self-check; boundaries-as-nervous-system-safety; handoff to coaching / Healing Circle.
- **Topic cluster:** trauma-patterns
- **Best placement:** companion on F7 (people-pleasing) and F9 (familiar vs safe); Resources library; a natural coaching feeder.
- **Follow-up pathway:** this is the one deeper-commitment magnet where an email invite is defensible (higher intent, natural nurture into coaching). Invite, do not require; keep the download reachable. Nurture via Sunday Letter, or a short Kajabi sequence if/when she moves automation there.
- **Clinical review:** Yes. Relational-trauma content checked; explicitly not couples therapy; non-diagnostic.
- **Access:** Open by default; email-invited variant acceptable given intent level.

- **Keep open (frictionless, trust-first, acute-adjacent):** #1 Pattern Map, #2 Emotional Flashback First-Aid, #4 Hypervigilance or Intuition, #5 Vocabulary Guide, #6 Coming Back After a Trigger, #7 Creative Regulation Toolkit. These match the existing all-open library and protect a vulnerable reader from hitting a form at a bad moment.
- **Email-invited (honest, because email is the medium or the intent is higher):** #3 the 5-day series (email-native) and, optionally, #8 the relationships workbook. Always offer an open fallback so gating is never deceptive.
- **Biggest platform decision:** the 5-day series forces a choice. Substack cannot drip it. Recommend running email automation on Kajabi (already owned) or MailerLite/Kit; otherwise ship it as a single open PDF and skip automation. Do not promise a "5-day sequence" while on Substack-only, because the platform cannot deliver it.
- **Clinical review is required (not optional) for #2, #5, #6, and thorough for #8**, because those formats most invite self-diagnosis or acute-moment use. All eight carry the standing scope disclaimer.
