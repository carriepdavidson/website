Nervous System Regulation

They're Selling You a Quarterly Reset. My Body Doesn't Know It's Q3.

Your nervous system doesn't keep the calendar. It keeps the record.

By Carrie Davidson, BSPH, BSN, RN, Registered nurse and certified trauma recovery coach
Published June 29, 2026 · 5 min read

This article is trauma recovery education, not therapy, diagnosis, or medical treatment. It is not a substitute for care from a licensed mental-health or medical professional. If you are working with a therapist or physician, let this walk beside that care. It does not replace it.

No one tells you that you can't buy your way out of your own nervous system. They sure are trying though.

The big wellness word this year is reset. Have you noticed? The Great Nervous System Reset. That's the actual trend, the thing the spas and the retreats and the very calm women on my feed are selling in 2026. The pitch is that the era of the thirty-second hack is over, and the new luxury is consistency. Monthly tune-ups. Quarterly reset intensives. A weekend, a sound bath, a breathwork container, and you come out the other side regulated. Recalibrated. Brand new.

And I want to be careful here because some of it is real. Rest is real. Nature is real medicine, the science on that is solid now. I'm not mad at a weekend in the woods.

I'm mad at the word reset.

Let me tell you about the time I bought it. A couple years into my own healing, doing well enough to have a credit card and not enough to know better, I booked the thing. Four hundred dollars. A "nervous system reset intensive." I'm lying on a bolster in a room that smells like palo santo, a woman is running a mallet around a crystal bowl, and everyone around me has gone soft and heavy into the floor the way you're supposed to. And I am scanning. I'm clocking the exits. I'm aware of the man breathing two mats over. My jaw will not unclench. My body is lying very still and doing the math it has always done, which is who is in this room and how fast can I get out. I paid four hundred dollars to lie on the floor and stay on guard. And I remember thinking, with this awful flat clarity: it doesn't know we're safe. It doesn't care that I paid.

It doesn't know we're safe. It doesn't care that I paid.

Here's the clinical truth, and this is the part the trend leaves out. A nervous system is not a phone. You do not power it down and power it back up clean. The body runs something researchers call allostatic load, the accumulated wear of a system that has been bracing for too long. Mine started bracing at six. That set point, that baseline hum of this is not safe, was not installed at a spa and it does not uninstall at one. Underneath your awareness, the body is running neuroception, constantly, scanning face and tone and room for threat, deciding without asking you whether to drop into the ventral vagal state where rest and connection actually live. A survivor's neuroception is calibrated to a childhood. It reads a quiet room and a stranger's breath as a problem to solve. A weekend cannot argue with twenty years of evidence. You cannot intensive your way past that. Believe me, I tried, with a bowl singing two feet from my head.

And here is the pivot, the thing it took me years and a credit card to learn. Regulation was never going to be an event. It is the least sexy thing in the world. It is a thousand boring repetitions of small safety that the body slowly, grudgingly, comes to believe. Going to bed at the same time so the animal in me learns the night is not an ambush. Eating before I'm starving. Coming back to the same five people who don't flinch. Feeling the panic rise and staying anyway, so my body collects one more piece of evidence that the fear passed and I lived. That's it. That's the whole protocol. It's free and it's daily and no one can sell it to you in a weekend because it isn't a product. It's a relationship. You are slowly, patiently, becoming someone your own body can trust.

So have you. You don't need a kidnapping or a nursing degree for this. If you've ever come home from the relaxing vacation more wound up than when you left, if you've ever wondered why the breathwork everyone swears by leaves you bracing, if your body has ever refused to power down on the exact schedule the wellness internet promised, then you already know. Your nervous system doesn't keep the calendar. It keeps the record. And a record gets rewritten one ordinary day at a time, not four times a year on the company plan.

I'm not telling you this regulated. I'm telling you this on a Monday where I woke up at five with my heart going for no reason a clock could explain. I still scan rooms. I still flinch. I don't do the boring practice perfectly and I never will. But I do it most days now, scared or not, and somewhere in there my body has started, just barely, to believe me.

This one's for the women who paid for the reset and laid on the floor still bracing. The high-functioning ones, the ones who can survive anything and are exhausted from proving it. You are not behind. You are not doing it wrong. You don't need another intensive. You need a thousand quiet ordinary days where nothing bad happens and you're finally there to notice. Start with this one.

If any of this brought up something heavy, be gentle with yourself today. And if you're in real distress, please reach out to someone you trust or a professional who can sit with you in it. You don't have to reset alone. You just have to take the next ordinary day.

Carrie Davidson, BSPH, BSN, RN

Registered nurse and certified trauma recovery coach; the nurse who became her own patient. She writes about the overlap of complex trauma and addiction: the same wound, telling itself in two different languages. Author of the memoir Addicted to Trauma.
