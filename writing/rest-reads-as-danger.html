Nervous System Regulation

Everyone Keeps Telling Me to Rest. My Body Reads Rest as the Threat.

Stillness is the moment the predator gets you.

By Carrie Davidson, BSPH, BSN, RN · Registered nurse & certified trauma recovery coach
Published June 11, 2026 · 5 min read

This article is trauma recovery education, not therapy, diagnosis, or medical treatment. It is not a substitute for care from a licensed mental-health or medical professional. If you are working with a therapist or physician, let this walk beside that care. It does not replace it.

The whole internet has decided rest is the medicine. Rest is allowed. You're safe now, lie down, let the nervous system settle. And every time I read it I want to throw my phone across the room, because nobody tells you the part where rest is the scariest thing your body has ever been asked to do.

Here's what they don't say. For some of us, stillness isn't relief. Stillness is the moment the predator gets you. So when the wellness people say just rest, sweet girl, what my body hears is lie down and stop watching. And everything in me says absolutely not.

Let me tell you about a specific night. I'm on the couch. Nothing is wrong. Dishes done, doors locked, kid asleep, the kind of quiet most people would call peace. And my heart is going like I'm being chased. No reason. No thought I can point to. Just my whole system bracing for a hit that isn't coming, in a room where I am completely safe, on a Tuesday, in my own house. I sat there with my hand on my own sternum like I was trying to hold a door shut from the inside.

So let me be precise, because I'm a nurse and I'm not going to hand you a vibe and call it science. There's a difference between trauma that happened to you once and trauma that raised you. When the thing happens in childhood, before you have words, your nervous system doesn't file it as an event. It files it as the weather. It builds the whole house around the assumption that danger is the baseline and safety is the trick. So the wiring that should switch off when the threat passes never gets the memo that the threat passed, because for you it never did. Your baseline cortisol sits high. Your amygdala, the smoke alarm in the brain, gets calibrated in a burning building and then spends the rest of your life going off at toast. And here's the cruel part. Rest requires the smoke alarm to stand down. Rest requires you to stop scanning. For a body that learned safety was the lie and vigilance was the only thing that ever kept you alive, being asked to stop scanning doesn't feel like rest. It feels like being asked to take your hands off the wheel at seventy.

That's the thing nobody puts on the infographic. Hypervigilance isn't a bad habit I picked up. It's the thing that worked. It kept me alive when I was small and the world was not safe and no one was coming. My body did its job. It did it so well that it never learned how to stop.

And it took me a long time to stop being at war with that. To stop reading my own racing heart as proof I was broken. Because here is what I actually believe now, after years of being inside this and not on the other side of it. The vigilance was never the enemy. The vigilance is loyalty. It's the oldest part of me still standing guard over the little girl who needed somebody to. I do not get to shame that part out of existence. I get to walk over, slowly, and tell it the war is over. Not by forcing stillness. Not by white-knuckling a meditation app while my chest screams. But by giving my body one true scrap of evidence at a time that this room, right now, is actually safe. The settling comes after the proof. Never before it.

You cannot affirmation your way past a nervous system that has receipts.

So when you sit down to rest and your whole body revolts, hear me. You are not failing at healing. You are not bad at peace. Your alarm is just doing the thing it was built to do, in a building that isn't on fire anymore, and it doesn't know that yet. It's going to take more than one quiet Tuesday to convince it. It's going to take a thousand of them. And that's not you being broken. That's you being someone who survived something that required this much armor, now learning, in real time, that you're allowed to set a little of it down.

I'm still learning it. I want to be so honest with you about that. I did not write this from a hammock, regulated and serene, reporting back from the finish line. I wrote it from inside the climb, on a day my own body still flinched at quiet. I would not lie to you about this, because I am living it right alongside you.

This one's for the women who never got to be little. The ones who were the responsible one, the calm one in the crisis, the kid who learned to read the room before she could read a book. The ones who built whole lives out of staying ready. You proved you can survive anything. Y'all already know that. The next thing, the harder thing, is letting your body slowly find out that it doesn't have to anymore. Not all at once. Just one safe Tuesday at a time.

Carrie Davidson, BSPH, BSN, RN

Registered nurse and certified trauma recovery coach; the nurse who became her own patient. She writes about the overlap of complex trauma and addiction: the same wound, telling itself in two different languages. Author of the memoir Addicted to Trauma.
