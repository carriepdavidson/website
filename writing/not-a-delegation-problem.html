You Don't Have a Delegation Problem. You Have a Trust Injury.

Hypervigilance is what a nervous system builds when it learns early that no one is coming.

By Carrie Davidson, BSPH, BSN, RN, Registered nurse and certified trauma recovery coach
Published June 30, 2026 · 5 min read

This article is trauma recovery education, not therapy, diagnosis, or medical treatment. It is not a substitute for care from a licensed mental-health or medical professional. If you are working with a therapist or physician, let this walk beside that care. It does not replace it.

No one tells you that the thing burning you out is the same thing that built it. They keep handing you the wrong fix.

There's a study going around this week. Sixty-three percent of women founders say burnout is their single biggest challenge. Not money. Not the market. Burnout. And buried in the write-up is the line that actually got me, the part nobody puts on the slide: women founders are carrying a disproportionate share of the work that keeps everything running but never makes it onto a task list. The invisible stuff. The remembering. The double-checking. The I'll just handle it because it's faster than explaining.

And the advice, of course, is delegate. Build systems. Let go. As if you hadn't thought of that. As if you weren't trying.

Let me tell you why you can't.

Back when I was nursing, I'd hand off a patient at the end of a shift, full report, everything documented, and then I'd go home and lie in bed at one in the morning running it again. Did I tell her about the line. Did I flag the potassium. I'd think about driving back. Not because the next nurse was bad. She was good. I knew she was good. It didn't matter. My body did not believe that anyone else would catch it, so my body kept holding it, off the clock, in the dark, for free. I was an exceptional nurse partly because of this. I caught the things people missed. I was also dying a little, every shift, from never once getting to set it down.

Here's the clinical truth, and this is the part the burnout study can't say out loud. What you're calling a delegation problem is hypervigilance. It's not a personality quirk and it's not control issues, which is the insulting little phrase people reach for. Hypervigilance is what a nervous system builds when it learns early that no one is coming. When you are a kid and the adult who's supposed to be watching is not watching, your brain does the only smart thing available to it. It promotes you. It puts a six-year-old in charge of threat detection and it never demotes her. The amygdala stays lit. The scanning never stops. You become the one who notices everything, anticipates everything, holds everything, because the one time you didn't, something happened, and your body wrote that down in permanent ink.

And then you grow up and you build a business, and that wiring is incredible at it. Of course it is. You see the problem three moves out. You remember what everyone forgot. You overfunction so reliably that an entire company learns to relax because you never will. That is not a flaw. That is a trauma response that found a job. The reason you can't hand it off isn't that you're a bad delegator. It's that handing it off requires your body to believe someone else will catch it, and your body has years of evidence that no one does. That's not a logic problem. You can't argue it out of yourself. It's a trust injury, and it happened long before you ever filed an LLC.

That is not a flaw. That is a trauma response that found a job.

Here's the pivot, the thing it took me a collapse to learn. The hypervigilance was never the enemy. It is the single most powerful instrument I have. The eye that scans a room for danger is the same eye that reads a client's face and knows what she isn't saying. The brain that couldn't trust anyone to catch it is the brain that catches everything. I don't want it gone. I'd be ordinary without it. What I needed wasn't to kill the engine. It was to build the off switch the engine never came with. To practice, on purpose, the unbearable thing, letting someone else hold a piece of it and not checking. Watching them not drop it. Collecting one boring piece of evidence at a time that the world does not, in fact, end when I am not personally bracing against it.

So have you. You don't need a nursing badge or a childhood like mine for this. If you've ever redone the thing it would've taken less time to just let go, if you've ever lain awake holding a problem that was already solved, if you've been called too controlling by people who have no idea what it cost you to be that reliable, then you already know. You don't have a discipline problem. You have a nervous system that learned, way too young, that safety was a thing you manufactured yourself or did without.

I'm not telling you this from the other side of it. I delegated something real last week and then opened my laptop at eleven to check it, like an addict, hand already on the mouse. I closed it. Barely. That's the whole work right now: closing the laptop, sitting in the wrongness, letting the night pass without me guarding it. Some days I can. Plenty I can't.

This one's for the founders, the over-functioners, the women who became the safest pair of hands in every room they were ever scared in. You are not too much. You are not failing at letting go. You are running the most expensive software ever installed in you, and it's keeping you up at one in the morning the same way it kept me. You don't need to delete it. You need to teach it, slowly, that you're not six anymore, and someone other than you is finally allowed to catch the fall.

If any of this brought up something heavy, be gentle with yourself today. And if you're carrying more than feels survivable, please reach out to someone you trust or a professional who can help you hold it. You were never supposed to catch everything alone.

Carrie Davidson, BSPH, BSN, RN

Registered nurse and certified trauma recovery coach; the nurse who became her own patient. She writes about the overlap of complex trauma and addiction: the same wound, telling itself in two different languages. Author of the memoir Addicted to Trauma.
