The PTSD and the Addiction Were the Same Animal Wearing Two Name Tags

You don't get addicted to the substance. You get addicted to finally not feeling it.

By Carrie Davidson, BSPH, BSN, RN, Registered nurse and certified trauma recovery coach
Published June 24, 2026 · 5 min read

This article is trauma recovery education, not therapy, diagnosis, or medical treatment. It is not a substitute for care from a licensed mental-health or medical professional. If you are working with a therapist or physician, let this walk beside that care. It does not replace it.

It's PTSD Awareness Month. You've seen the posts. The soft graphics, the lists of symptoms, the reminders that help is available. And I'm glad for all of it, I am. But there's a thing nobody says out loud in awareness month, and I've decided I'm done waiting for someone else to say it.

For a lot of us the PTSD and the addiction were never two separate things. They were the same animal. It just wore two different name tags depending on which doctor was looking.

They put the trauma in one department and the addiction in another. One gets a sympathy ribbon. The other gets a side-eye and a lecture about choices. And I lived right at the seam where those two stories are supposed to be kept apart, so let me tell you what it actually looked like from the inside.

It looked like a girl who could not get the volume in her own head to go down. The memories didn't arrive like memories. They arrived like weather, like the room temperature changing, like my body deciding without my permission that the danger from twenty years ago was happening again right now in a grocery store parking lot. Hypervigilance doesn't take breaks. The nervous system that learned to scan for threat at five years old does not retire when the threat is gone. It just keeps scanning an empty room. And I would have done almost anything, paid almost anything, to make it stop for one hour. So I did. The substance wasn't the point. The off switch was the point. I wasn't chasing a high. I was chasing the first quiet I'd felt in my body in years.

Here's the clinical truth, and I want the recovery rooms and the awareness-month people to really sit in this one. They call it the self-medication hypothesis, and it is not soft science, it's one of the most replicated findings in the whole field. PTSD and substance use disorder co-occur at staggering rates, and it isn't a coincidence, and it isn't two unlucky diagnoses landing on the same person. It's cause and response. A traumatized brain runs high on cortisol and adrenaline, the amygdala stuck in the on position, the prefrontal cortex, the part that's supposed to say you're safe now, knocked offline by the flood. Alcohol, opioids, the whole pharmacy, they work on that exact system. They do, chemically, for a few hours, what your own nervous system forgot how to do. They turn the alarm off. You don't get addicted to the substance. You get addicted to finally not feeling it. That's not a character flaw. That's a person with no other tool reaching for the only one that worked.

And here's what naming it that way did for me. It took the substance out of the center of the story and put the wound back where it belonged. For years I thought I was a weak person who couldn't stop. That's the story they hand you, and you wear it, and it eats you. The day someone helped me see that the using was a symptom and the trauma was the disease, something in my chest unclenched that had been clenched my whole life. Not because it let me off the hook. Because it finally pointed me at the right thing. You cannot white-knuckle your way out of a pain you were never allowed to feel. You have to go back and feel it, with help, with a regulated body, slowly. The substance was never the enemy. It was a terrible, brilliant, life-saving answer to a question nobody ever helped me ask.

So have you. If you medicated something. The wine that was never about the wine. The work you can't stop doing. The scrolling, the eating, the people you keep going back to. Whatever turned the volume down so you could survive a day. That wasn't weakness. That was your body doing the only pain management it had access to, and I'd like us to stop being ashamed of how hard we tried to stay alive.

Now here's the part I'm still living, and I'd be lying to you if I tied this up neat. People ask me if the wanting ever fully goes away. The pull toward the off switch. And I don't know. I really don't. Some mornings it's a whisper and some mornings it's louder, and I have built a life with enough actual safety in it that I don't need the chemical quiet the way I used to. But I won't stand on a stage and tell you it disappeared, because mine hasn't, and I would not lie to you because I'm living it. What I can tell you is that I'm not afraid of it the way I was. I know its name now. That changes everything and it changes nothing, and both of those are true at the same time.

This one's for the ones with two name tags. The trauma survivors who also got the other label, the harder one, the one people whisper. You were not two broken things. You were one nervous system doing its best with what it had. I see you. It's awareness month. Consider yourself seen.

Carrie Davidson, BSPH, BSN, RN

Registered nurse and certified trauma recovery coach; the nurse who became her own patient. She writes about the overlap of complex trauma and addiction: the same wound, telling itself in two different languages. Author of the memoir Addicted to Trauma.
