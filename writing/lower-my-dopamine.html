They Told Me to Lower My Dopamine. They Didn't Know What I'd Done to Get It.

You were never too much. You were under-fueled.

By Carrie Davidson, BSPH, BSN, RN, Registered nurse and certified trauma recovery coach
Published June 23, 2026 · 4 min read

This article is trauma recovery education, not therapy, diagnosis, or medical treatment. It is not a substitute for care from a licensed mental-health or medical professional. If you are working with a therapist or physician, let this walk beside that care. It does not replace it.

The dopamine detox is back. You've seen it. Thirty days off the good stuff, no sugar, no scrolling, no sex, no noise, just you and a glass of water and the gray. Reset your reward circuitry, they say. Get your baseline back. And every time it comes around I want to ask the person selling it one question. What if the baseline is the thing that's trying to kill me?

Because here's what nobody asks before they tell you to lower your dopamine. They never ask what you had to do to feel anything in the first place.

I'll tell you what I did. I chased it. For years I chased intensity like it was oxygen, because to me it was. The substance was just the most efficient delivery system, but it was never really about the substance. It was about the volume. I needed the world turned up. I needed the chaos, the crisis, the edge, the next thing, the danger that made me feel switched-on and real and here. Calm felt like a held breath. Quiet felt like the second before something breaks. And a so-called normal life, the flat gray peaceful one they want me to detox my way back to, that felt like being buried alive with my eyes open. So I reached for whatever turned the dial back up. That's not a moral failure. That's a girl who learned a long time ago that stillness is when the bad thing happens.

Here's the clinical truth, and I need the recovery people especially to hear this one. A brain raised in chronic danger does not set its baseline where a safe brain sets it. The reward system, the dopamine circuitry that's supposed to give you a little lift from a good meal or a warm room, it gets recalibrated upward by years of living in survival. Ordinary good doesn't register. The receptors got blunted by a childhood spent flooded with stress chemistry, so the small stuff doesn't touch the sides. You need a bigger hit just to feel normal, and normal for us was never neutral. It was hypervigilance with the lights on. So we go looking for the thing that meets the body where it already lives. That's the actual engine under a lot of addiction in trauma survivors. It isn't weakness. It's a reward system that got its dial broken in a house that broke it.

So when the wellness world hands me a dopamine fast, what they're really prescribing is thirty days of making myself feel dead on purpose. And for some people that's a reset. For me, early on, that gray was the most dangerous place on earth. The numb was where I'd disappear.

Here's what actually moved me, and it took me years to find it. The answer was never to starve the intensity out of myself. That brain isn't going anywhere. I didn't get to detox my way into being a calm, low-key, easy person, and I've stopped grieving that. The work was learning to feed the intensity something that doesn't cost me my life. Same engine. Different fuel. I run hot, so I built a life that needs heat. Hard conversations. Real work that demands everything. A body I move until it's tired. Loving people at full volume instead of numbing down to tolerate them. The crisis brain doesn't get smaller, but you can hand it something true to burn, instead of waiting for it to find something that burns you.

Same engine. Different fuel.

And if you've been told your whole life that you're too much... too intense, too fast, too loud, too needy, too on... I want you to sit with the possibility that the thing they shamed is the same thing that kept you alive. You didn't get addicted because you were broken. You got addicted because you were built to chase a high baseline and the world handed you the cheapest version of it first. That intensity isn't the bug. Unmanaged, it nearly killed me. Pointed somewhere real, it's the engine under everything I've built.

So no, I'm not going to lower my dopamine. I'm going to stop spending it on things that empty me out.

I'm not telling you this from the other side of it. I still feel the pull toward the high-volume life every single day, and some days I want the old fast cheap version so bad I can taste it. I'm not healed. I'm just finally honest about what I'm working with. This one's for the women who were called too much. The intense ones. The ones in recovery who were told to want less and somehow felt more broken for not being able to. You were never too much. You were under-fueled. Go find something real to burn.

Carrie Davidson, BSPH, BSN, RN

Registered nurse and certified trauma recovery coach; the nurse who became her own patient. She writes about the overlap of complex trauma and addiction: the same wound, telling itself in two different languages. Author of the memoir Addicted to Trauma.
