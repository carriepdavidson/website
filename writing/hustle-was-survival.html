The Hustle Was Your First Survival Skill. Of Course You Can't Put It Down.

The hustle was never a habit. It was the first coping skill you ever built.

By Carrie Davidson, BSPH, BSN, RN, Registered nurse and certified trauma recovery coach
Published June 16, 2026 · 5 min read

This article is trauma recovery education, not therapy, diagnosis, or medical treatment. It is not a substitute for care from a licensed mental-health or medical professional. If you are working with a therapist or physician, let this walk beside that care. It does not replace it.

No one tells you that the burnout isn't coming from the business.

They tell you it's the calendar. The boundaries you didn't set. The team you didn't hire. There's a stat going around right now, sixty-three percent of women founders saying burnout is the biggest thing they're up against, and the whole internet answered it the same week. Delegate more. Build a self-care ritual. Get a morning routine, a virtual assistant, a meditation app, a softer to-do list. And I read all of it nodding, thinking, sure, fine, none of that is wrong... and also none of that is going to touch the actual thing. Because the actual thing started long before you ever had a business to burn out from.

Here's where I felt it first. I was a nurse who could not, would not, leave a shift undone. Twelve hours turning into fourteen. Somebody needed something, I was already moving. I was the one who stayed. The one you could count on when the place was on fire. And everyone around me called it dedication, called it heart, and I let them, because the truth was uglier and I wasn't ready to say it. I wasn't staying because I was so generous. I was staying because being needed was the only place I had ever felt safe. If I was indispensable, I couldn't be discarded. That was the math. I learned it long before nursing. I learned it as a little girl who figured out that being useful, being good, being the one who handled it, was how you earned the right to stay in the room.

Let me say the clinical part, because this is where I need you to hear me as the nurse and not just the woman who's been through it. What the burnout researchers are quietly admitting in 2026 is that the pattern is older than the job. The belief running the whole engine is "I'm valuable when I achieve," and that belief doesn't get installed in a board meeting. It gets installed in childhood. A nervous system that learned early that love was conditional, that safety had to be earned, that the price of belonging was performance, does not file that away as a memory. It wires it in as an operating system. The clinical word for what you're doing when you overwork past every reasonable limit is not ambition. It's pattern repetition. You are running a survival strategy that once kept you safe, in a present that no longer requires it, and your body cannot tell the difference. Cortisol doesn't know it's 2026. It thinks you're still seven and the achievement is the only thing standing between you and being left.

So that's why the productivity advice slides right off. You cannot delegate your way out of a wound. You cannot build a morning routine sturdy enough to convince a body that never once believed it was safe to stop. The hustle was never a habit. It was the first coping skill you ever built, and you built it young, and it worked. That's the part nobody says. It worked. It kept you alive in a house where rest wasn't safe. Of course you can't put it down. You're not weak for white-knuckling it. You're loyal to the strategy that saved you.

And here's the turn, the thing I'm actually living. The skill that saved you is not the same skill that lets you live. Surviving and thriving are not the same country, and the passport between them is not a better calendar. It's the willingness to feel the old terror underneath the overwork, to stay with your seven-year-old self long enough to tell her the room won't empty if she sits down. That's the work. So much slower and less Instagrammable than a self-care ritual. It's grief, mostly. Grieving that you had to earn your place at all.

The skill that saved you is not the same skill that lets you live.

If you have built something real while quietly running on fumes, hear me. You are not lazy and you are not broken and you do not need another system. You survived something, and survival made you formidable, and now it's asking to be more than survival. The exhaustion you're feeling isn't failure. It's your body telling you the strategy expired and you outgrew it.

I want to be honest about where I'm standing in this. I am not writing to you cured, with my boundaries gleaming and my evenings free. I still catch myself reaching to be the one who stays, the one who handles it, the one who earns it. I still feel the pull to prove I'm worth keeping. I'm not on the far side of this. I'm in it, learning in real time that I was always allowed to stay, even when I wasn't producing anything at all. Some days I believe it. Plenty of days I'm just practicing.

This one's for the founders, the over-functioners, the women who learned to be indispensable before they learned to be held. The ones who built an empire out of a survival skill and are only now feeling what it cost. You were never too much. You were just keeping yourself safe the only way you were ever taught.

Pick up the pen, y'all. The hustle kept you alive. It's allowed to retire now.

Carrie
