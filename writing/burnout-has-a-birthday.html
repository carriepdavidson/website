Your Burnout Has a Birthday. It's Older Than Your Business.

You cannot delegate a nervous system.

By Carrie Davidson, BSPH, BSN, RN, Registered nurse and certified trauma recovery coach
Published June 19, 2026 · 5 min read

This article is trauma recovery education, not therapy, diagnosis, or medical treatment. It is not a substitute for care from a licensed mental-health or medical professional. If you are working with a therapist or physician, let this walk beside that care. It does not replace it.

There's a number going around this month and I can't stop chewing on it. Sixty-three percent of women founders say burnout is their biggest challenge. Not funding. Not sales. Not the algorithm. Burnout. And every article about it says the same careful thing... women carry a disproportionate share of the work that keeps everything running but never makes it onto a task list. They call it the invisible load. They prescribe delegation. Boundaries. Cycle syncing. A virtual assistant. And I want to be careful here because none of that is bad advice. But I read it and something in my chest goes... y'all think this started with the business?

Let me tell you when mine started. I was six and I was the one who knew. I knew which door slam meant go to your room and which one meant nothing. I knew the temperature of the house from the sound of a key in the lock. I knew whose mood was about to tip before they did, and I arranged myself accordingly, small and useful and three steps ahead. That was the job. Nobody handed me a task list. The list was the whole house and it lived in my body, every minute, and the consequence for dropping an item was not a missed deadline. So no. The invisible load did not start when I registered an LLC. It started before I could spell my own name.

Here's the clinical part, and I want you to actually keep this one. What they're calling the mental load, in a nervous system that grew up in danger, is hypervigilance. It's not a personality quirk and it's not poor time management. It's a brain that learned, early and without words, that safety depends on tracking everything. The amygdala gets trained to treat unmonitored space as a threat, so it never fully powers down. It keeps a tab open on every person, every tone, every unfinished thing, because the original lesson was that the thing you didn't watch was the thing that hurt you. That scanning costs energy. Real, metabolic, cortisol-soaked energy, burned around the clock by a body that does not believe it is allowed to stop. Call that founder burnout if you want. The nurse in me calls it an alarm system that was installed in childhood and never got the message that the fire is out.

And here's why the delegation advice slides right off women like us. You cannot delegate a nervous system. You can hire the assistant. You can hand off the inbox, the calendar, the books. And then you will lie awake at 2am tracking all of it anyway, because the watching was never really about the tasks. The watching is how your body has felt safe since before you had a choice in the matter. Hand off the work and the vigilance just finds something new to guard. I have done this. I have built the perfect system and then stood inside it still braced, still scanning, still waiting for the door to slam, and wondered what was wrong with me that the calm I engineered didn't reach my chest.

But here is the turn, and it took me a long time to earn it. That same wiring is the reason I am extraordinary at what I do. The girl who read the whole room became a nurse who caught the thing nobody else caught. The vigilance that exhausted me is the exact thing that made me trusted, capable, the one people call when it's bad. My intensity was never the problem. My intensity, unmanaged, running with no off switch and no one telling it the war is over, that's the problem. The gift and the wound are the same circuit. Nobody tells you that in a burnout-prevention webinar.

The gift and the wound are the same circuit.

So if you're in that sixty-three percent, hear me. Your burnout has a birthday and it's older than your business. The thing draining you is not a scheduling flaw and you will not boundary your way out of it, because it isn't really about the work. It's a survival skill that kept a child alive and never got told it could retire. So have you. You learned to hold the whole house. Then you grew up and built a house big enough to need holding, and called the exhaustion a founder problem. It's not. It's a six-year-old who's still on watch.

I'm not telling you this from some delegated, regulated mountaintop. I still wake up at 2am running the list. I still catch my body guarding a room that is completely safe. I don't have the off switch figured out, and I won't pretend I do. But I've stopped fighting the wiring and started talking to it. Thanking it. Telling it, slowly and a thousand times, that it did its job and it's allowed to set the watch down now. So this one's for the founders, the nurses, the moms, the women who run everything and can't figure out why they're so tired. You're not weak and you're not behind. You've just been on shift since you were a kid. It's okay to clock out. Start there.

Carrie Davidson, BSPH, BSN, RN

Registered nurse and certified trauma recovery coach; the nurse who became her own patient. She writes about the overlap of complex trauma and addiction: the same wound, telling itself in two different languages. Author of the memoir Addicted to Trauma.
