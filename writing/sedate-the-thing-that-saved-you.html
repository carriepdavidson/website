They Are Teaching You to Sedate the Thing That Saved You

The intensity was never the problem.

By Carrie Davidson, BSPH, BSN, RN, Registered nurse and certified trauma recovery coach
Published July 12, 2026 · 5 min read

This article is trauma recovery education, not therapy, diagnosis, or medical treatment. It is not a substitute for care from a licensed mental-health or medical professional. If you are working with a therapist or physician, let this walk beside that care. It does not replace it.

Everyone's got a calm nervous system for sale this year.

Scroll for ten seconds and someone's telling you your dysregulation is the reason your life feels the way it feels. Regulate. Reset. Come back to baseline. There's a whole industry now built on the idea that the goal is calm, and if you're not calm, something in you is broken and needs fixing. 2026 decided the nervous system is the new self-care and now every coach with a ring light is a polyvagal expert.

And here's the thing... some of it is real. I'm a nurse. I'm not going to sit here and tell you the science is fake. Your body does keep the score. Trauma does live in the tissue, not just the memory. When I say I couldn't breathe in certain rooms, I mean my diaphragm actually changed how it moved. That's not a metaphor. That's real.

But I need to say the other thing too, because nobody's saying it and it's costing women their power.

They are teaching you to sedate the very thing that kept you alive.

Let me get specific. For most of my life I was told I was too much. Too fast. Too intense. Too loud in the room. Talk slower. Calm down. Why are you always going a hundred miles an hour. I heard it from teachers, from partners, from people who loved me and meant well. And I spent years believing them, believing that the speed in me was a malfunction. That if I could just get regulated enough, quiet enough, still enough, I'd finally be okay.

Here's what was actually happening in my body. My nervous system learned early that the world was not safe, so it built me an engine. Hypervigilance. A brain that scans a room the second I walk in and knows where every exit is, who's angry, who's about to break, what's about to go wrong. Cortisol on tap. A system that runs hot and fast because for a long time, running hot and fast is what stood between me and the thing that was going to hurt me.

That same engine made me an exceptional nurse. The one who caught the patient going bad before the monitor did. The one who stayed calm on the outside while running seventeen scenarios underneath it. The intensity they told me to fix in the classroom is the exact thing that saved lives on the floor. Same wiring. Different room.

So when I hear the whole internet telling women to regulate, to soften, to get quiet, to bring the nervous system down down down to some flat calm baseline, I feel that in my body. Because there's a difference between a nervous system that's on fire and a nervous system that just runs hot, and the wellness industry has decided they're the same thing. They're not.

A dysregulated nervous system that's genuinely suffering, that's flooded, dissociating, shut down, unable to sleep or feel or function, yes. That needs care. That needs regulation. I'm not arguing with that. I've been there and I climbed out and I'll help you climb out too.

But a fast one? An intense one? A system that needs a degree of friction to feel alive, that thrives in organized chaos, that lights up under pressure that would flatten someone else? That's not dysregulation. That's your operating system. And when you take everything they're selling and try to file it down into stillness, you don't heal. You just amputate the strongest part of yourself and call it growth.

I know because I tried. I tried to become a calm person. I bought the version of healing where the goal was a woman who never spiked, never surged, never felt the room. And I got quieter and I got smaller and I got so bored I could feel myself disappearing. That's not regulated. That's sedated. There's a difference, and your body knows it even when your feed doesn't.

That's not regulated. That's sedated.

Here's what I'm actually working toward now, and I'll tell you honestly I don't have it fully figured out, I'm still in it. It's not calm. It's aimed. It's taking this fast, intense, hot-running system I was shamed for my whole life and pointing it at something instead of pretending I can turn it off. The intensity was never the problem. Unmanaged, unnamed, pointed at nothing, it burns the house down. Named and aimed, it's the engine underneath everything I've ever built.

So if you're the woman who's been told to slow down her whole life, who's been handed the calm-nervous-system gospel and felt something in you quietly refuse... you're not resisting your healing. You might be protecting the one part of you that already knows how to survive anything. Don't let anyone talk you into sedating it just because they never learned how to hold a woman who runs this hot.

You were never too much. You were just never handed to someone who could see the engine for what it was.

Carrie Davidson, BSPH, BSN, RN

Registered nurse and certified trauma recovery coach; the nurse who became her own patient. She writes about the overlap of complex trauma and addiction: the same wound, telling itself in two different languages. Author of the memoir Addicted to Trauma.
