Nervous System Regulation

Regulated Doesn't Mean Quiet. It Means You Have Range.

Regulation is not sedation. Regulation is range.

By Carrie Davidson, BSPH, BSN, RN, Registered nurse and certified trauma recovery coach
Published June 15, 2026 · 5 min read

This article is trauma recovery education, not therapy, diagnosis, or medical treatment. It is not a substitute for care from a licensed mental-health or medical professional. If you are working with a therapist or physician, let this walk beside that care. It does not replace it.

Regulate your nervous system. That's the whole gospel this year.

Get calm. Slow down. Soften your jaw. Breathe from your belly. Book your monthly nervous-system tune-up like it's a bikini wax. Every wellness account I follow has decided 2026 is the year of the Great Nervous System Reset, and I'm here for most of it, I really am. But I keep hearing one quiet thing humming underneath all of it, aimed straight at women like me. Be less. Be softer. Be still. Bring the volume down on the whole of who you are.

And I want to push back on that, because here's what I know about myself. I thrive in chaos. Organized chaos, but chaos nonetheless. My brain does not light up in the stillness. It lights up in the surge.

Let me tell you where I first felt that. I was a nurse in rooms where everything was on fire. A body coding, alarms going, six people moving at once and somebody has to be the still center of all that noise, and it turned out that somebody was me. While my whole system flooded, my hands got slow. My voice got level. I could see the entire room at once, every monitor, every face, the exact half-second something was about to go wrong. The thing the world had always called too much, too intense, too fast, that was the exact thing that made me good at keeping people alive. I was not calm in there. I was on. There is a difference, and the difference is the whole point.

Here's the nurse part I need you to actually hear, because the internet is getting this wrong. A regulated nervous system is not a quiet one. Regulation is not sedation. Regulation is range. It's the capacity to ramp all the way up when the moment demands it and then come all the way back down when it's over. The clinical word is flexibility. A healthy system can mobilize hard, full throttle, sympathetic floor it, and then find the brake and settle. That settling is the vagal tone everybody's selling you. But they keep skipping the first half. Dysregulation isn't intensity. Dysregulation is getting stuck. Stuck on, wired and unable to land. Stuck off, numb and unable to rise. The problem was never that I run hot. The problem was that for years I couldn't come down. I only had the gas. I was missing the brake.

So when the wellness world flattens "regulated" down to "calm," it makes a quiet promise that a woman like me should become smaller, slower, softer, and call that healed. And I have to be honest about what that sounds like in my body. It sounds exactly like every teacher, every boyfriend, every boss who ever told me to settle down. It is the old shame in a linen outfit. Calm down, sweetheart. Now with a meditation app.

The reset I actually needed was never to dim the engine. It was to install the brake. To keep all my intensity, the fire that makes me a good nurse and a good coach and a person who can walk into someone's worst day and know exactly what it needs, and add the part where I get to choose when it runs and when it rests. That's regulation. Not less voltage. Better wiring.

Not less voltage. Better wiring.

So if you've spent your life being told you're too much, hear me. Too intense. Too loud. Too fast. Too sensitive, too driven, too on. That intensity was never the malfunction. It is the engine underneath everything you have ever built. You don't need somebody to talk you down into a smaller version of yourself and call it peace. You need range. You need the come-down to finally work as well as the come-up always has.

I want to be honest about where I'm standing. I am not on the far side of this, regulated and serene, posting from a sunlit corner. I still overshoot. I still catch myself wired at midnight with no off switch in sight, my body convinced the surge is the only safe place to live. I'm not learning to be calm. I'm learning to be free, which is a different thing. I'm learning the landing, not the shutdown. Some days I stick it. Plenty of days I don't.

This one's for the intense women. The too-muches. The ones who run hot and got handed a lifetime of permission slips to please be quieter. You are not a problem to be sedated. You're an engine that was never taught where the brake was. Keep the fire. Build the brake. Both, at the same time, are yours.

Pick up the pen, y'all. You were never too much. You were just running without the second pedal.
