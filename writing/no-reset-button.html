Nervous System Regulation

There's No Reset Button. Some of Us Never Had Factory Settings.

Some of us were never issued a before.

By Carrie Davidson, BSPH, BSN, RN, Registered nurse and certified trauma recovery coach
Published June 17, 2026 · 5 min read

This article is trauma recovery education, not therapy, diagnosis, or medical treatment. It is not a substitute for care from a licensed mental-health or medical professional. If you are working with a therapist or physician, let this walk beside that care. It does not replace it.

Everybody wants to reset their nervous system this year.

That's the word now. Reset. It's everywhere. There's a retreat in Baja, there's a 30-day protocol, there's a whole prediction that 2026 is the year of the Great Nervous System Reset, and the pitch is always the same... calm the autonomic nervous system, get out of fight-or-flight, and everything else falls into place. Sleep. Cold plunge. Forest bathing. Regular meals. And listen, none of that is wrong. I do half of it. But the word reset has been bugging me for weeks, and I finally figured out why. Reset means going back. Back to factory settings. Back to before. And some of us were never issued a before.

Here's what I mean. I have spent a lot of my life trying to find the version of me that existed prior to the damage, like there was a clean original underneath all of it if I could just scrub hard enough. There wasn't. By the time I was six I was already the adult in my own house. By the time I was a young woman I had been taken, renamed, made into someone called Grace so I could survive a situation a child should never have to survive. There is no factory setting under that. The setting was installed in the danger. The danger is the original. So when somebody hands me a protocol that promises to return my nervous system to baseline, my whole body goes... return it to what, exactly? You can't restore a default that was never there.

Let me say the clinical part, because this is the part I actually want you to keep. A nervous system is not a phone. It does not ship with a neutral operating state that gets corrupted by bad experiences and can be wiped clean. It is built. It develops in relationship, in real time, in response to whatever world it actually lands in. A baby whose cries are answered builds a nervous system that expects answering. A baby who learns the world is unpredictable and unsafe builds one that expects threat, and that expectation gets laid down in the amygdala, in the vagal tone, in the cortisol rhythm, before there are any words to argue with it. That is not a malfunction. That is the system working exactly as designed, encoding the environment it was given. You cannot reset a thing whose baseline was danger. There is no earlier save file. The danger was the first draft.

The danger was the first draft.

So I had to put the word down. And what I picked up instead was not reset. It was renegotiate. I am not going back to a calm original me, because she doesn't exist. I am sitting down with the nervous system I actually have, the one that learned to scan a room before it learned to trust one, and I am slowly, repeatedly, showing it new evidence. Not a 30-day reset. A 30-year renegotiation. Some mornings I show my body that the quiet isn't a trap. Some mornings I fail and the old wiring wins and I'm braced for impact over absolutely nothing. And then I do it again the next day. That's not failure. That's the actual work, and the actual work does not photograph well, which is exactly why nobody's selling it to you in Baja.

If you came up the way I came up, you already know this in your body even if you've never said it out loud. You've tried the reset. You've done the cleanse, the retreat, the protocol, and you've felt that flicker of calm and then watched it evaporate the second real life walked back in, and you blamed yourself. You decided you were the one who couldn't be fixed. You weren't broken. You were just being sold restoration when what you actually needed was renegotiation, and those are not the same thing, and the difference is everything. So have you. You have a nervous system that did its job. It kept you alive in a place that wasn't safe. It is not waiting to be wiped. It is waiting to be shown, gently and a thousand times, that the danger is over now.

I'm not telling you this from the far side of healed. I'm not. I still flinch at quiet. I still catch my body bracing on a normal Tuesday. I don't know if there's a finish line and I've stopped pretending I can see one. But I went from wanting to not wake up to looking forward to the morning, and I did that without ever once resetting, because there was nothing to reset to. I just kept renegotiating. So this one's for the women who keep buying the reset and keep coming up empty. You were never the broken product. There was no factory. There's just you, and the body that carried you here, and the slow holy work of teaching it that it's safe to set the watch down. That's not a reset. That's a relationship. Start there.

Carrie Davidson, BSPH, BSN, RN

Registered nurse and certified trauma recovery coach; the nurse who became her own patient. She writes about the overlap of complex trauma and addiction: the same wound, telling itself in two different languages. Author of the memoir Addicted to Trauma.
