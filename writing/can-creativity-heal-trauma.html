Creativity as medicine

Can Creativity Heal Trauma? What Making Things Does for a Trauma-Trained Nervous System

Survival mode and creative flow draw on the same account. You only get to spend the balance in one place.

By Carrie Davidson, BSPH, BSN, RN · Registered nurse & certified trauma recovery coach
Published July 14, 2026 · 9 min read

The short, honest answer

Can creativity heal trauma? Yes, creativity can be a genuine part of trauma healing, and no, it is not the whole of it. Creative practice gives a trauma-trained nervous system something no amount of insight can: new, felt experiences of agency, completion, and safety. That is exactly the kind of evidence a stuck pattern needs. But creativity is a companion to good support, not a substitute for treatment when treatment is needed. Anyone selling you a paintbrush as a cure is selling you something.

I want to give you the honest version, because you have probably already met the two dishonest ones. The first says creativity is decoration: nice for people who have time, irrelevant to serious healing. The second says creativity is everything: buy the journal, do the course, manifest the healed self. Both miss what actually happens in the body when a person who learned to survive starts, carefully, to make things instead.

I did not come to this as a theory. I came to it as a nurse who understood her own patterns in clinical detail and could not stop living them, until making things became part of how I practiced being safe. Creation is the C in the method I now teach, and it is there on purpose: not as a reward at the end of healing, but as one of its engines.

Trauma and creativity share one account

Here is the frame that makes everything else make sense. Hypervigilance is expensive. Scanning every room, reading every face, rehearsing every conversation, managing everyone's moods: a trauma-trained nervous system spends a staggering share of its daily resources on defense. Survival mode and creative flow cannot run at the same time, because they draw on the same account.

This is why "just be more creative" is useless advice for a dysregulated body, and it is why creativity returning is one of the most reliable signs that regulation is actually happening. When the loop starts to loosen, energy comes back. The question becomes what to do with it. Creation is the answer that builds forward instead of circling back.

Creation is anything you move toward instead of away from.

It also explains something many survivors quietly grieve: the creative self they lost, or never got to meet. If you stopped drawing, writing, singing, or playing somewhere around the age the hard things happened, that is not a coincidence and it is not laziness. The account was drained by staying alive. The good news is written into the same math: as safety grows, the balance returns, and the capacity you think you lost is usually still there, waiting.

What the research actually suggests

I am a nurse, so I will not overclaim. The research on creative and arts-based practices for trauma is promising and still maturing. Reviews of arts-based interventions and expressive writing report meaningful reductions in trauma symptoms for many participants, and creative practices are increasingly used alongside established treatments. Studies vary in size, design, and population, so the honest summary is this: the evidence is encouraging, the mechanisms are plausible, and creative practice is best understood as a complement to professional care rather than a standalone treatment.

That is a weaker sentence than a marketing page would print, and I am comfortable with that. Because the case for creative healing does not rest on any single study. It rests on how well the practice matches what we know about trauma itself: that it lives in the body, that it narrows a person's sense of agency, and that it heals through new experience rather than new information. Making things delivers new experience, on demand, in doses you control.

Why making things regulates the body

Nothing about this is mystical. The mechanisms are ordinary, and they stack.

Rhythm and repetition settle the body. Kneading dough, knitting a row, sanding wood, laying down a brushstroke: repetitive, bilateral, predictable movement is one of the oldest regulation tools humans have. Your body does not need to understand why it is settling. It settles.

Focused making anchors you in the present. Trauma pulls attention backward into memory and forward into threat. A making task pulls it here, to the wet paint, the next stitch, the line you are writing. Presence is not a mindset for a trauma survivor. It is a location, and creation gives your attention somewhere to live.

Externalizing creates distance. When something inside you becomes a thing outside you, a page, a collage, a melody, you can look at it instead of only being it. That small gap between you and the pattern is the same gap the witnessing practice builds. The page holds what your body has been holding, and for a few minutes your body gets to put it down.

Finishing something is evidence. This one matters most for the loop. A trauma-trained body is full of proof that nothing it does changes anything. Completing a small made thing, however imperfect, deposits the opposite proof: I chose, I shaped, I finished. Agency is not an idea you can be argued into. It is an experience your body has to have, and creation manufactures that experience in miniature, every time.

What counts as creative (almost everything)

If the word creative makes you flinch because you cannot draw, hear this clearly: creative healing has nothing to do with talent, and the product does not matter. The benefit lives entirely in the making.

Cooking something without a recipe counts. A garden counts. Journaling counts, badly and honestly, in a notebook no one will read. Rearranging a room counts. Humming in the car, building a playlist that says what you cannot, taking one photograph a day, fixing something with your hands. A relationship where you show up as yourself is a creative act. So is a slow Saturday morning that does not have to be earned.

Creation is anything you move toward instead of away from. That is the entire entry requirement.

How to start when creating feels unsafe

One warning, offered with love, because it surprises almost everyone: creating can feel threatening at first. Unfamiliar reads as unsafe to a trauma-trained body, and making something is deeply unfamiliar to a system organized around defense. Add the perfectionism most survivors carry, where anything less than excellent feels like exposure, and the blank page can trigger the exact loop you are trying to loosen.

So start smaller than feels reasonable.

Ten minutes, once, this week. Not a practice, not a commitment, one visit. Choose something with low stakes and some texture or rhythm to it: tearing and gluing paper, writing three sentences about what your body feels like right now, watering and rearranging the plants. Let it be bad on purpose. If your inner critic arrives, and it will, you do not have to argue with it. Notice it the way you notice any trigger: with curiosity instead of judgment. That noticing is Stage 1 of the whole method, happening at a table with glue on your fingers.

No hustle, no shame, no urgency. Wanting things is a skill. Making things is how it grows.

When creativity is not enough

Here is the boundary, stated plainly, because this is where a page like this earns or loses your trust. Creative practice is powerful and it has limits. If you are in acute crisis, if you need diagnosis or medication, or if trauma is overwhelming your ability to function, you need a licensed mental-health professional, and creative practice should walk beside that care, not stand in for it. A collage is not a treatment plan. It was never supposed to be.

What creativity offers is different and, in its own lane, irreplaceable: a daily, self-directed way to give your nervous system new evidence, rebuild agency in small doses, and practice being a person who makes things rather than only a person who survives things. That practice belongs to you. No one can prescribe it, and no one can take it away.

Slowly coming back to yourself: that is what this stage of healing is actually called. The making is how you walk there.

Frequently asked questions

Can creativity heal trauma?

Creativity can be a powerful part of trauma healing, though it is not a replacement for professional treatment when treatment is needed. Creative practice gives a trauma-trained nervous system new experiences of agency, completion, and safety, which is exactly the evidence a stuck pattern needs. Emerging research on arts-based and expressive practices supports what survivors have long reported: making things helps.

Do I need to be artistic for creative healing to work?

No. Creative healing has nothing to do with talent or producing something good. The benefit lives in the making, not the product: choosing, shaping, finishing. Cooking, gardening, journaling, rearranging a room, and humming in the car all count.

Is this the same as art therapy?

No. Art therapy is a clinical treatment delivered by a credentialed art therapist. Creative practice as described here is a self-directed, non-clinical daily practice that can complement therapy. The two can work beautifully together.

Why does making things calm the nervous system?

Several ordinary mechanisms stack together: repetitive motion and rhythm are naturally settling, focused making anchors attention in the present, externalizing what is inside you creates distance from it, and finishing something small gives the body a felt experience of agency and completion.

Can creativity replace therapy?

No. If you need diagnosis, medication, or deep trauma processing, that is a licensed professional's work, and creative practice should walk beside it, not replace it. If you are in crisis right now, please reach out: call or text 988 (US), or text HOME to 741741.
