Nervous System Regulation

They Keep Calling It High-Functioning. Nobody Asks if I Can Feel Anything.

Survival is not the same as being alive.

By Carrie Davidson, BSPH, BSN, RN · Registered nurse & certified trauma recovery coach
Published June 10, 2026 · 6 min read

This article is trauma recovery education, not therapy, diagnosis, or medical treatment. It is not a substitute for care from a licensed mental-health or medical professional. If you are working with a therapist or physician, let this walk beside that care. It does not replace it.

There's a thing happening right now where everybody finally has a name for the people who fall apart. And I'm glad. I am. But nobody's naming the other ones. The ones who don't fall apart. The ones who answer every email and show up to every shift and hit every deadline and look, from the outside, like the most put-together person in the room... and feel absolutely nothing while they do it.

That's me. That's been me for years. And there's finally a word for it that's making the rounds this year. Functional freeze. And the first time I read it I had to put my phone down because who tf gave them permission to describe the inside of my chest like that.

Here's what it is. You know fight, you know flight. There's a third one underneath those, and it's the one nobody romanticizes because it doesn't look dramatic. It's the shutdown. Freeze. And functional freeze is the cruelest version of it, because the body goes dark but the lights stay on. You keep performing. You keep producing. You keep being the reliable one, the strong one, the one who's handling it. And inside, the lights are off. You're running the whole operation from a body nobody's home in.

Let me be precise, because I'm a nurse and I'm not going to hand you a mood and call it a diagnosis. There's a part of your nervous system called the dorsal vagal branch. It's old. It's the oldest part of the survival wiring, older than fight, older than flight. When a threat feels too big to fight and there's nowhere to run, the body does the last thing it has. It powers down. It conserves. It pulls you up and out of yourself so that whatever's coming won't hurt as much when it lands. Animals do it when the predator has them. They go still. They leave. We call it dissociation when it happens to people, and here's the part that wrecked me: it does not require a present-day predator. A body that learned to shut down young will keep doing it on schedule for the rest of your life, in meetings, in your own kitchen, in the middle of a hug, for no reason at all except that the wiring is still set to the old emergency.

I learned to do this so early I thought it was my personality. I thought I was just calm under pressure. I thought I was just not a feelings person. I built a whole career on a nervous system that had figured out how to keep my hands working while it took the rest of me offline. I was a phenomenal nurse partly because of it. You cannot fall apart with your hands inside someone's worst day. So the freeze made me good. It made me employable. It made me the one everybody leaned on. And the cost of that, the bill that comes due, is that you wake up one day successful and steady and respected and you realize you have not felt the inside of your own life in years.

You've been watching it from the parking lot.

And this is the thing I need the high-functioning ones to hear, because nobody's going to come check on you. Nobody checks on the person who's handling it. That's the trap. You are so good at functioning that your shutdown is invisible, even to the people who love you, even to you. You'll call it being tired. You'll call it being busy. You'll call it being focused. You'll call it anything except what it is, which is that your body decided a long time ago that feeling things wasn't safe, and it has been quietly protecting you from your own life ever since.

Survival is not the same as being alive. I have to say that plainly because I spent most of my life unable to tell the difference. I survived everything. I have the receipts. And surviving so hard, for so long, so well, is exactly the thing that can keep you from ever actually living, because the machine that kept you alive does not know the war is over. It's still pulling you out of your body to protect you from a threat that left the building decades ago.

So coming back is not a productivity hack. It's not a cold plunge. It's the slow, unsexy, almost unbearable work of letting yourself feel one real thing at a time and not leaving when it gets big. It's noticing you've gone numb and not shaming yourself for it, because that numbness saved your actual life once. It's grief, mostly. You have to grieve all the years you were in the parking lot. And you have to thank the part of you that drove you out there, because she wasn't broken. She was brilliant. She just never got told it was safe to come back inside.

I'm not all the way back in my body, y'all. I want to be honest about that. I still catch myself in the parking lot. I still go numb and have to walk myself back, hand on my own chest, this is real, you're allowed to be here, the thing is over. I'm not writing to you from the other side of it. I'm writing to you from the work.

This one's for the high-functioning ones. The ones nobody worries about. The nurses, the founders, the firstborn daughters, the women who built whole lives on a body that learned to leave. You're not cold. You're not a robot. You're not "just not emotional." You're a person who got so good at surviving that you forgot you were allowed to be home. Come back inside. Slow. One real feeling at a time.

Pick up the pen, y'all. You're allowed to feel this one.

Carrie
