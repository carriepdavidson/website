Nervous System Regulation

They Figured Out How to Sell You Back Your Own Nervous System

The healing isn't somewhere you go. It's not for sale.

By Carrie Davidson, BSPH, BSN, RN, Registered nurse and certified trauma recovery coach
Published June 13, 2026 · 6 min read

This article is trauma recovery education, not therapy, diagnosis, or medical treatment. It is not a substitute for care from a licensed mental-health or medical professional. If you are working with a therapist or physician, let this walk beside that care. It does not replace it.

So the big wellness prediction for this year is finally out and it's the "great nervous system reset." Quarterly reset intensives. Monthly nervous system tune-ups. Weekly sauna. Healing as a luxury subscription you renew like a gym membership you'll feel guilty about in March. And I read all of it and I just sat there thinking... they did it. They actually did it. They found the last thing I had that nobody had figured out how to charge me for, and they put it behind a paywall in a linen kaftan.

I'm not even mad about the sauna. The sauna's fine. I'm mad about the sentence underneath the sauna, which is the same sentence the wellness industry has been selling me my whole life, just wearing new clothes: your regulation is a thing you buy, a thing you go somewhere to get, a thing that happens at a destination you are currently not at and will need to purchase access to. That's the lie. That's the whole lie. And I know it's the lie because I tried to buy my way out of my own body for about a decade and I have the credit card statements to prove it didn't take.

Let me be a nurse for a second. Here's what's actually true about your nervous system, the unsexy version nobody can sell. Regulation is not a state you arrive at. It's not a place. It's a capacity, and it's built the same boring way you build any capacity in the body, which is reps. Tiny ones. Your vagus nerve, the big wandering one that runs from your brainstem down through your heart and your gut, it's the brake pedal on your whole stress response. And like any system in the body, it responds to frequency, not intensity. A long exhale at the kitchen sink. Cold water on your face for ten seconds. Your own hand on your own chest at 2am saying the thing is over. That's the rep. That's what actually moves the dial. Not the quarterly intensive. The quarterly intensive feels incredible and then you come home to the exact same nervous system you left with, because you can't outsource a daily practice to a once-a-season event any more than you can get fit by going to one really good yoga retreat in Baja and then not moving until fall.

And here's the part that makes me actually angry, the part that's personal. The people most likely to buy the reset retreat are the women who need the daily practice the most and trust themselves with it the least. Trauma does that. When your body learned early that safety came from outside you, from reading the room, from managing other people, from getting it right, you grow up genuinely not believing that anything as cheap and as close as your own breath could possibly be the thing that saves you. It has to be expensive. It has to be hard to get to. It has to be administered by someone with a certification and a beautiful website, because surely the thing that fixes a body this broken can't be free and can't be something I already have. I lived inside that exact belief. I'd have paid anything. I did pay almost anything. What I would not do, for years, was sit still in my own kitchen and trust that one slow breath, done again tomorrow, and again the day after, was real medicine.

It is, though. That's the thing. It's the least marketable true thing I know. You cannot put "breathe out slowly, twice a day, for the rest of your life, mostly when nothing dramatic is happening" on a sales page. It's not a transformation, it's a maintenance. It's flossing for your soul. And the industry can't sell flossing, so it sells the reset, the intensive, the before-and-after, the destination, and quietly leaves out the part where the destination is a Tuesday and you're already standing in it.

It's flossing for your soul.

So have you. If you've ever bought the program, the retreat, the supplement, the course, the membership, looking for the thing that would finally regulate you from the outside, and felt that hot little flush of shame when it didn't stick, hear me: it didn't stick because regulation was never a thing out there. You're not bad at healing. You just got sold a destination when the truth was a practice, and a practice is humbler and slower and so much less photogenic, and it's also the only thing that has ever actually worked.

I want to be honest about where I'm writing this from. I am not a regulated person who figured it out and is now reporting back from the calm. I still chase the shiny outside fix. I still catch myself adding the expensive thing to the cart at midnight, convinced this one's different. I'm doing the boring reps and I miss days and I start over. I'm not on the other side of this. I'm in it with you, hand on my own chest, exhaling on purpose, mostly believing it.

This one's for the women with the full cart and the empty practice. The ones who'll fly across the country for a reset and won't give themselves ten quiet seconds at the sink. The healing isn't somewhere you go. It's not for sale. It's already in your body, it's free, and it's asking for almost nothing except that you come back to it tomorrow.

Pick up the pen, y'all. The good stuff was never at the destination.

Carrie
