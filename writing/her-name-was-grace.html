Her Name Was Grace. She Took the Worst of It So I Wouldn't Have To.

Grace was not my illness. Grace was my anesthesia.

By Carrie Davidson, BSPH, BSN, RN, Registered nurse and certified trauma recovery coach
Published June 27, 2026 · 5 min read

This article is trauma recovery education, not therapy, diagnosis, or medical treatment. It is not a substitute for care from a licensed mental-health or medical professional. If you are working with a therapist or physician, let this walk beside that care. It does not replace it.

No one tells you that dissociation isn't checking out. They talk about it like it's a glitch. A symptom on a checklist. The thing your therapist gently names when you go quiet and far away in the middle of a sentence. Spacey. Dissociative. Like your brain just... left the building and forgot to leave a note.

But I was there when mine did it. And I need to tell you what it actually is, because the wellness internet has it so wrong it makes me want to scream.

When I was a kid, I got taken. I'm not going to give you the whole thing today, the where and the how long, because that's not the part that matters. The part that matters is what my brain did to keep me alive inside it. It made someone else. Her name was Grace. Grace was calm. Grace was agreeable. Grace could smile at the person who was hurting her and feel almost nothing while she did it, because nothing was the only thing that was survivable. Carrie was somewhere else. Carrie got to leave. Grace stayed and took it.

I used to think that meant I was crazy. That something in me had cracked down the middle. It took me years and a nursing degree and a lot of reading to understand that what happened to me was not a break. It was a build.

Here's the clinical truth, and I want you to actually hear this one. Dissociation is the nervous system's most advanced survival move. When you can't fight and you can't run, when the threat is bigger than you and there is no door, the brain does the only thing left. It splits the experience off from the self who can't afford to feel it. Depersonalization, where you watch your own life like it's happening to a stranger. Derealization, where the room goes flat and dreamlike and far. In the most extreme version, what the research now calls structural dissociation, the personality itself partitions. One part carries the terror and the memory so another part can keep going to school, keep being a daughter, keep functioning like the floor is solid. That's not weakness. That is a six-year-old's brain performing emergency surgery on her own consciousness with no anesthesia and no help, and getting it right. Grace was not my illness. Grace was my anesthesia. She was the most loyal thing my body ever built.

And here is what took me the longest to understand. The same brain that learned to leave is the brain that almost couldn't come back. Because dissociation doesn't know when the danger's over. It got so good at vanishing that for years it would pull the cord at the smallest thing. A raised voice. A bad memory. Intimacy, of all things, which is supposed to be safe. I'd be in the middle of my own life and feel myself go to the ceiling, watching, gone. The very thing that saved me became the thing standing between me and being here. That's the cruel little joke of trauma. The survival skill outlives the thing it survived.

The survival skill outlives the thing it survived.

So have you. Maybe not Grace. Maybe you didn't get a name. But if you've ever driven somewhere and arrived with no memory of the drive, ever floated up out of a hard conversation and watched yourself have it, ever gone strangely calm and far away exactly when you should've been falling apart, then you know this. Your brain has done it too. It left so you could stay. Everyone who has survived something they couldn't escape has a Grace somewhere. We just don't all have the language for her yet.

The work now isn't to get rid of her. I'm done trying to delete the parts of me that did the hardest jobs. The work is to let her know it's over. To notice the moment I start to float and put my feet on the actual floor and say, out loud sometimes, we're not there anymore, we made it, you can come back. Some days it works. Some days I lose whole afternoons to a fog I didn't choose and I have to forgive myself for a nervous system that's still flinching at a war that ended decades ago.

I'm not telling you this from the far side of healed. I went away again last week. Something small, and I was gone before I clocked it. I'm not writing to you as a woman who fixed her dissociation. I'm writing as a woman who finally stopped hating herself for it. There's a difference, and it might be the whole difference.

This one's for the ones who go away. The floaters, the foggy ones, the women who've been called spacey or cold or checked out by people who have no idea what it cost to build that exit. You weren't broken. You were brilliant, under conditions no one should ever have to be brilliant in. Thank the part of you that left. Then, gently, start showing her the way back.

Carrie Davidson, BSPH, BSN, RN

Registered nurse and certified trauma recovery coach; the nurse who became her own patient. She writes about the overlap of complex trauma and addiction: the same wound, telling itself in two different languages. Author of the memoir Addicted to Trauma.
