Trauma recovery coaching for nurses and caregivers

The same vigilance that can make you exceptional at your job may be costing you outside of it.

BSN, RN · Certified Trauma Recovery Coach · Author of Addicted to Trauma

I am a registered nurse (BSPH, BSN, RN) and certified trauma recovery coach, and I say this as someone who has been the clinician and the patient both. I know the particular exhaustion of being the steady one, the capable one, the person everyone else leans on. And I know how quietly the cost of that can build, long after the badge comes off.

You might recognize this

When the skills that keep you safe at work do not switch off

Not everyone in a caregiving role carries trauma, and this is not a diagnosis of you. But if some of this feels familiar, it might be worth your attention.

You might still be reading a room for danger hours after you have left the unit. You might scan a restaurant from the doorway before you sit, checking the exits without deciding to. You might be the one everyone relies on, the person who holds it together for the family, the friends, the whole shift, and quietly wonder who holds it for you.

You might come home with nothing left. Not the kind of tired that sleep touches, but hollowed out, the way a body gets when it has been on alert for too long. And somewhere in there, the creative life you used to have may have gone quiet: the writing, the music, the making, the parts of you that had nothing to do with being useful. You might have started to confuse being useful with being worthy, until rest itself began to feel like something you had to earn.

If you recognize yourself here, you are not doing it wrong. A nervous system that learned to stay ready does not simply stand down because the shift ended. It is doing exactly what it was trained to do, until it is given new evidence.

A little language for it

There are names for some of what caregivers carry

The helping professions have language for part of this, and it can help just to know the words exist. Compassion fatigue describes the slow erosion that can come from caring for people in pain, day after day. Secondary traumatic stress describes what can happen when you absorb the details of other people’s worst moments as part of the job. These are recognized, well-documented experiences. Naming them is not a diagnosis; it is only language, offered so you have something more precise than “I’m just tired.” Burnout is the word most people reach for, and it is real, but it is not always the whole story.

Because sometimes there is a quieter question underneath, and only you can answer it: was some of the vigilance already there, long before the first shift? For some people, the work did not create the pattern. It found one that was already running, and gave it somewhere to be useful. Is that true for you? You do not have to know the answer today. You only have to be willing to ask.

The way back

Creativity is how you find your way back to yourself

You cannot reason your way back from this. The return happens in the body, and for a lot of us that path runs straight through the creative life that went quiet. Creativity is the opposite of stuck. It is not a luxury you get to once everything else is handled; it can be the very way a nervous system learns, in the body, that it is safe to do something other than brace.

That is the heart of the Conscious Creation Method: a nervous-system-informed way of seeing the pattern, building the pause that interrupts it, and slowly making something new, until the making itself becomes the evidence that a different life is possible. It is not about becoming an artist. It is about coming back to the parts of you that the job asked you to set down.

I do not coach from a podium. I coach from the floor I once found myself on, now standing.

Where the edges are

What this is, and what it is not

I want the boundary to be clear, because you of all people know why it matters.

This is coaching and education, present-and-forward companionship as you build a new pattern at the pace your nervous system can hold. My RN background informs how I understand trauma and the body, but coaching is not nursing, medical treatment, or clinical care, and it does not diagnose or treat anything. If you are in acute crisis, or you need diagnosis, medication, or treatment, licensed care comes first, and coaching can walk beside it.

This work is trauma recovery coaching and education. It is not therapy, diagnosis, or medical treatment, and it is not a substitute for care from a licensed mental-health or medical professional. It can walk beside that care. It does not replace it. If you are in crisis, the resources at the bottom of this page can help right now.
