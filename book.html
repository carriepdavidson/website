What the book is about

What is Addicted to Trauma about?

Addicted to Trauma is a memoir built on a single, unifying idea: trauma and addiction are not two separate problems. They are the same wound, telling itself in two different languages. The reaching for the drink, the drama, the overwork, the chaos — it is not a moral failure layered on top of an old hurt. It is the old hurt, still speaking, still trying to keep a frightened nervous system alive the only way it learned how.

Carrie writes from both sides of that sentence. As a registered nurse, she was trained to recognize the clinical machinery — the HPA axis, the cortisol, the implicit memory that runs beneath conscious thought. As a woman in the loop, she lived what no chart could hold: what it is to explain your own patterns fluently at 3 p.m. and be swallowed by them at 3 a.m. The book moves between those two vantage points — the nurse and the patient, the map and the territory — until they become one story.

The loop is not a character flaw. It is a nervous system doing exactly what it was trained to do — until you give it new evidence.

It is a redemption story, but not a tidy one. There is no single rock bottom, no lightning-bolt recovery. There is the slower, truer work of coming back to yourself — of learning that you do not have to think your way out, you have to feel your way home. If you have read every book, done every modality, and still felt insight-rich and transformation-poor, this memoir is the story of how that gap finally closed.

Themes

The threads running through it

Five ideas the book keeps returning to — the same ones that shape the coaching and the Method.

Trauma and addiction as one wound

The unified thesis at the center of everything: addiction understood not as the problem but as a symptom — a nervous system self-medicating a wound it was never allowed to feel.

Why calm can feel unsafe

When chaos is what your body learned to call home, peace registers as danger. The book sits inside that paradox — the pull toward drama, stress, and the familiar ache.

Insight is not the same as change

Understanding your patterns can feel like progress while the loops keep running. The memoir is honest about the years spent knowing exactly why — and still not being able to stop.

The clinician who couldn’t heal herself

What it costs to be the one who holds everyone else’s crisis while quietly drowning in your own — and what it takes to finally become your own patient.

The body as the way through

Bottom-up, not top-down. The turning point is not a better explanation but a nervous system given new evidence — slowly, repeatably — that safety is possible.

Coming home to yourself

Not a cure, but a homecoming. Learning to feel at home in your own body after a lifetime of leaving it. Companionship over expertise; agency over shame.

Who it’s for

Who should read this book?

If you recognize yourself here, this story was written for you.

  • The high-achieving woman who looks fine on the outside and is quietly exhausted by patterns she can name but can’t break.
  • The therapy veteran — years of talk, apps, breathwork, and workshops — who is tired of understanding herself and still repeating herself.
  • Anyone who suspects their “bad habits,” overworking, or attraction to chaos is really a trauma response wearing a disguise.
  • Readers of complex-trauma and addiction memoirs who want the science underneath the story, told by someone who lived it and was trained to understand it.
  • People who love someone in the loop and want to finally understand what it feels like from the inside.
