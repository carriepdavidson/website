Questions, answered plainly

Trauma recovery coaching questions, answered

Plain answers to the questions you’d type at 11pm.

Coach versus therapist, cost, whether you can heal without therapy, why the same patterns keep repeating: the honest answers below, from a registered nurse and certified trauma recovery coach. Each one stands on its own, so start wherever your question lives.

Frequently asked

The questions people actually ask

Last updated 21 July 2026 · Written by Carrie Davidson, BSPH, BSN, RN.

What is a trauma recovery coach?

A trauma recovery coach is a trained, non-clinical guide who helps you move present-and-forward: interrupting the patterns trauma left behind and building new ones through regulation, skills, and accountability. A coach does not diagnose, prescribe, or treat mental illness; that is a therapist’s role. A coach walks beside you in the doing.

What makes my version different is the RN behind it: I’m a registered nurse (BSPH, BSN) who trained in complex trauma and addiction, then lived the far side of both. Your patterns are not a flaw in you, and patterns can change.

What’s the difference between a trauma coach and a therapist?

The core difference is scope. A therapist is licensed to diagnose and treat mental-health conditions and can work with the past clinically. A trauma recovery coach is non-clinical and works present-and-forward: on patterns, nervous-system regulation, and action. Neither replaces the other; for many people they work best side by side.

Because I’m a registered nurse, I can hold the coaching lane while genuinely understanding the clinical one, but I still coach, I don’t treat. Insight alone does not always interrupt the pattern, and sometimes healing asks for a new experience in the body. There’s a full comparison table on the coaching page.

Can you heal trauma without therapy?

For some people, meaningful healing happens outside a therapist’s office: through somatic work, community, coaching, and consistent nervous-system regulation. But “without therapy” shouldn’t mean “without support.” If you have a trauma or mental-health diagnosis, therapy is often essential, and coaching is a complement, not a replacement. Healing is not a solo sport.

The real question isn’t therapy versus not: it’s whether you have the right support for where your body actually is.

My philosophy on money matches my philosophy on recovery: no scarcity theater, no urgency. If the ARC Program isn’t right yet, the free resources and The Sunday Letter are real places to begin.

Is trauma coaching right for me?

It’s likely a fit if you’re high-functioning, stable, and quietly exhausted by a loop you understand but can’t stop: a therapy veteran who understands her patterns but still can’t change them. It’s not the right first step if you’re in acute crisis or need diagnosis and treatment; that’s therapy or medical care. The first call is where we decide together, with no pressure to continue.

If it’s not the right fit, I’ll tell you, and point you toward what is. See more on the coaching page.

Why do I understand my trauma but still can’t change?

Because insight lives in the thinking brain, and trauma patterns live in the nervous system, which runs faster than thought. You can know exactly why you do something and still do it, because understanding a loop is not the same as teaching your body a new response. That gap is the space between knowing and doing.

This is the most common reason smart, self-aware women stay stuck. The way through isn’t more thinking; your body has to learn something different.

Why do I keep repeating the same patterns?

Because your nervous system is doing exactly what it was trained to do: running an old loop that once kept you safe, until it learns something safer. Repetition isn’t a character flaw or a lack of willpower; it’s a learned response firing faster than thought. Patterns change when the body, not just the mind, learns something new.

The loop is not a character flaw. It is a nervous system doing exactly what it was trained to do, until you give it new evidence.

How do I get out of survival mode?

You don’t force your way out of survival mode: you signal safety to your nervous system until it believes you. That means small, repeatable, body-based practices (breath, grounding, co-regulation) far more than mindset reframes. Survival mode is fight, flight, or freeze stuck on; the way out is repeated proof that the threat has passed.

It’s less a single breakthrough than a slow accumulation of moments where your body learns it’s safe to come down.

Why am I addicted to stress, chaos, or drama?

If chaos feels like home and calm feels unsafe, it’s usually not a personality flaw. It’s a trauma response. A nervous system raised on unpredictability can mistake stress for safety and read peace as a threat, even craving the cortisol of crisis. This is the trauma–addiction overlap: the same wound, telling itself in two languages.

It’s also the heart of my memoir, Addicted to Trauma. Learning to tolerate calm is some of the bravest work there is.

Can you have trauma and still be high functioning?

Absolutely. Many high-achieving women carry significant trauma while looking completely fine on the outside. High functioning often is the trauma response: perfectionism, over-working, and control can be survival strategies. Success can mask suffering so well that you don’t even call it trauma. Looking okay and feeling okay are not the same thing.

If your life looks enviable and still feels like a fight, you are not making it up, and you are not ungrateful.
