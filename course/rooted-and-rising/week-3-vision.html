A Letter to Begin: What Do You Actually Want?

Now the fun begins, and for many of us, the squirmy part too. This week I'm going to ask you what you actually want. Not what's realistic. Not what would be fine. What you want. If that question makes you flinch, you're in exactly the right place. Wanting is a skill, and like all skills, it returns with practice. Let's practice.

Desire Is a Compass, Not a Character Flaw

Somewhere along the way, most women learned that wanting is dangerous. Wanting too much makes you greedy, selfish, unrealistic, "a lot." So we learned to want in secret, or to pre-shrink our desires to a socially acceptable size, or to want only on behalf of other people.

Here's a reframe to sit with: your desire is data. The longing that keeps returning, across years, across circumstances, is not random appetite. It's your inner compass pointing at a life that would actually fit you. You can be talked out of a goal, but you can't be talked out of a calling; it just goes underground and waits.

Conscious creation doesn't begin with making a wish. It begins with telling yourself the truth about what you want, at full size, before the editing department gets hold of it.

Values First, Then Visuals

A vision built only from images (the house, the body, the business) collapses easily, because images are often borrowed. (Whose house is that on your vision board, really? Yours, or one the algorithm sold you?) A vision built on values holds, because values are yours.

Before we write the vision, we identify the felt experiences you're actually after. Freedom. Depth. Play. Safety. Significance. Beauty. Usually the house or the income is a delivery vehicle for three or four core felt-values. When you know what they are, two things happen: your vision gets honest, and the path gets wider, because there are a hundred ways to deliver "freedom and depth," and only one way to deliver "that exact house."

A Vision You Can Feel

The visions that change lives share two qualities: they are specific, and they are felt. Specific means sensory. What does Tuesday morning look like in this life? What do you hear, smell, wear, eat? Vague visions give the attention filter nothing to hunt for. Specific ones turn it into a bloodhound.

Felt means the vision lives in your body, not just on the page. When you read it, something in your chest should lift and ache at the same time: recognition. That feeling is the rehearsal. Every time you generate the feeling-state of the vision, you are practicing being the woman it belongs to. This is why we write it in present tense. Not as a lie, but as a fitting.

Which words hum? Choose up to five.

Tap the ones that produce the lift-ache feeling. They save privately in your browser, for your eyes only, and nothing is sent anywhere.

The Week 3 Practice: The Next Chapter Vision (45 minutes)

1. Ground (3 minutes), then read your three bridge beliefs aloud. You wrote them precisely so this exercise could go deeper than the old map would allow.

2. Values inventory. List ten moments in your life when you felt most alive. Look for the common threads. Distill three to five felt-values (single words: freedom, depth, play...).

3. The Tuesday letter. Set a timer for 20 minutes. Write, in present tense, an ordinary Tuesday in your life 18 months from now, a life built on those values. Wake to sleep. Sensory detail. No editing, no realism police. Let it be specific enough to film.

4. Body-test. Read it aloud. Mark the lines that produce the lift-ache feeling. Cut anything that reads as borrowed or performed.

5. Distill. Write a one-paragraph Vision Statement (5 to 7 sentences, present tense) capturing the essence. This paragraph becomes part of your daily anchor for the rest of the course.

Two things to know before you write. First: naming a true desire often surfaces grief about the years spent not naming it. Let it come; it's part of the wanting returning. Second: if your Tuesday letter looks suspiciously like your current Tuesday, ask gently: what did the editor remove? Then write one more paragraph with the editor off duty.

Your Daily Anchor + Affirmation (Week 3)

YOUR DAILY ANCHOR THIS WEEK (15 minutes each morning)

1. Breaths, orient, name your state: 4 minutes. 2. Read your Vision Statement aloud, slowly. Then close your eyes for two minutes and step inside one scene from your Tuesday letter. Generate the feeling, not just the picture. 3. Bridge beliefs + one line of evidence, as in Week 2.

AFFIRMATION OF THE WEEK

What I want is allowed to want me back.
