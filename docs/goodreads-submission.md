## Description (paste whole block)

A memoir of complex PTSD, addiction, queer love, and the body that finally refused to keep performing.

In a psychiatric hospital in southern California, a woman picks up a communal phone and leaves her mother a voicemail. She says her name is Grace. It is not.

So begins Addicted to Trauma, Carrie Davidson's unflinching memoir of the road that led to that white hallway, and the longer road she had to walk afterward. The first road began on a staircase at six years old, in a house where the adults could not be trusted to keep her safe. It ran through the elite schools where she performed her way to honors, through the relationships where she shapeshifted past her own memory of herself, through the substances that finally let her body put down its weapons. It ended, one Halloween night, with a missing persons report and a car crossing the border alone.

This is not a book about a kidnapping. The kidnapping is where the body finally stopped pretending. The harder work came after. Years of trying and relapsing, of building a sober life and watching parts of it dissolve under pressure, of nursing school and meeting halls and therapy sessions where her fifteen-year-old protector finally stepped aside. And then, when she had stopped expecting it, a relationship with a woman who taught her that love did not have to be earned.

A return to the body she had been leaving since she was six years old.

For readers of What My Bones Know, In the Realm of Hungry Ghosts, and The Body Keeps the Score.

## Author bio (short, for the author profile)

Carrie Davidson is a registered nurse (BSPH, BSN, RN), certified trauma recovery coach, writer, and artist. She specializes in the overlap of complex trauma and addiction: trained in the science of both, then lived the far side of both. Addicted to Trauma is her story of survival. She writes The Sunday Letter and coaches at carriepdavidson.com.
