# Research Verification

*Compiled 2026-07-13. Every scientific or statistical claim in Carrie's BrandStory, with a verification verdict and the exact wording that is safe to publish on carriepdavidson.com (a PUBLIC, YMYL site). No em-dashes. When in doubt, soften or cut. A tired reader trusting a health claim deserves a claim that holds up.*

## The standing rule (applies to every claim below)

Never state that the Conscious Creation Method is "backed by," "science-backed," "proven by," or "validated by" any study, unless that study evaluated the Method itself. None of the studies below evaluated Carrie's Method. They evaluated creative arts interventions in general.

**Preferred wording, reusable anywhere:**
> "The method is informed by research on nervous-system regulation, trauma, and creative arts interventions."

Any bio, method page, or pillar essay that currently says "backed by 2025 research" (Bio 4, Bio 6, Pillar 2, the Method premise) must be reworded to the informed-by phrasing.

## Claim (a): Nature Mental Health 2025 meta-analysis

**As written in the BrandStory:** "Research published in Nature Mental Health (2025) confirms creative arts interventions produce PTSD symptom reduction with effect sizes (Hedges' g = 0.85) comparable to first-line clinical treatments." Also cited as "33 studies, N = 4,587."

**Verdict: PARTIALLY VERIFIED. Real study, wrong applicability, unconfirmed figure.**

- The study is real: Applewhite et al., "Creative arts-based interventions for the improvement of PTSD symptoms in young people: a meta-analysis with a focus on non-Western populations," *Nature Mental Health* (2025). The 33-studies / N=4,587 figures match this paper. (A prior verification pass confirmed the citation; this pass re-confirmed the study exists and matches the study-count and sample-size figures.)
- **Applicability caveat:** the population is **young people, ages 3 to 18**, with diagnosed PTSD or trauma-related symptoms. Carrie's audience is adults. The study does not support a claim about adults, and it does not evaluate her Method.
- **The g = 0.85 figure was not independently confirmable** as the headline pooled effect for this paper. Do not publish the number.

**Safe wording used on-site (figure-free, applicability-honest):**
> "Emerging research on creative arts interventions points to meaningful reductions in trauma symptoms."

or, when more specificity is wanted:

> "A growing body of research suggests that creative, body-based approaches can help move trauma responses that talking alone does not reach."

Do NOT publish: the g=0.85 number, the word "confirms," "comparable to first-line clinical treatments," or any implication the finding was in adults.

## Claim (b): PMC 2025 meta-analysis, SMD -1.98

**As written in the BrandStory:** "2025 meta-analysis in PMC: standardized mean difference of -1.98 in PTSD symptom reduction. One of the largest effect sizes recorded for any non-pharmacological trauma intervention."

**Verdict: VERIFIED AS REAL, BUT STATISTICALLY FRAGILE. Do not publish the figure.**

- The study is real: "Colors of the mind: a meta-analysis of creative arts therapy as an approach for post-traumatic stress disorder intervention," *BMC Psychology* (January 2025). Indexed at PMC (PMC11725198) and published via BMC Psychology / Springer Nature (doi 10.1186/s40359-025-02361-4).
- The reported pooled result is **SMD = -1.98, 95% CI -3.8 to -0.16, p < 0.03, I2 = 98%**. So the -1.98 number is accurate to the source.
- **Why it is not safe to publish as a selling point:**
  - Only **7 studies**, adults with PTSD. Small evidence base.
  - The **95% CI (-3.8 to -0.16) is enormous and nearly crosses zero**. The true effect could be very large or barely present.
  - **I2 = 98%** is near-maximal heterogeneity. The studies disagree with each other so much that a single pooled number is close to meaningless as a headline figure.
  - The effect was **driven by drama therapy specifically**; music, art, and dance/movement showed less pronounced effects. Carrie's Method is not drama therapy.
  - The BrandStory's "one of the largest effect sizes recorded" framing over-reads a fragile, high-heterogeneity result.

**Safe wording used on-site:** none that cites this figure. Fold it into the same figure-free "emerging research" language as claim (a). If a source link is ever wanted, link the paper and describe it honestly as "a small 2025 meta-analysis of creative arts therapy for PTSD," never as proof.
